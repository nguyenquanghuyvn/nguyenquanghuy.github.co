> **In one sentence:** Paying local leaders to spread useful farming advice can support organic adoption, but rewards must be matched by verification, reputation, or penalties that make exaggerated claims costly.

## Why peer advice matters

Farmers often learn about unfamiliar practices from people they already know: experienced neighbors, cooperative leaders, demonstration farmers, or early adopters. This can be especially important for organic farming, which requires local knowledge and may perform differently across seasons and production conditions.

But peer advice raises a difficult question. The informed farmer may know more about the quality of a practice than the farmer who must decide whether to adopt it. A recommendation can therefore be helpful, incomplete, or overly optimistic—and the receiver may not discover the difference until after facing a bad production season.

This paper develops a theoretical model to study when peer advice remains credible.

## A simple distinction: resilient and fragile practices

The model considers two organic practices.

An **improved practice** is resilient. It performs better than conventional farming under both normal and extreme production conditions.

A **common practice** is fragile. It performs better in normal conditions but worse than conventional farming when conditions become extreme.

The local leader knows which type of practice is available. The follower does not. The follower hears the leader's message and then decides whether to adopt before the production conditions are known.

## Where the credibility problem comes from

Suppose the leader receives a share of the follower's positive gain when adoption succeeds. This can reward useful information. However, it can also create a temptation to describe a fragile practice as improved.

The reason is that the follower bears the loss when extreme conditions occur, while the leader is rewarded mainly when the outcome is positive. The leader may therefore benefit from encouraging adoption without fully bearing the downside risk created by poor advice.

The model identifies two conditions for successful information diffusion:

1. **The adoption condition.** After hearing the message, the follower must expect adoption to be better than remaining conventional.
2. **The truth-telling condition.** The cost of making a false claim must be at least as large as the additional reward the leader could obtain by exaggerating the practice's quality.

When both conditions hold, good practices can diffuse and bad advice is deterred. When credibility is weak, two failures are possible: farmers may adopt fragile practices too readily, or they may stop trusting peer advice and reject genuinely resilient practices.

## Rewards alone are not enough

The model's main lesson is that stronger incentives have two sides. A larger reward can motivate local leaders to share valuable information, but it also increases the payoff from exaggeration. Verification and accountability therefore need to become stronger as rewards increase.

## What this means for agricultural programs

The model suggests several practical principles for extension services, cooperatives, certification programs, and demonstration networks:

- **Verify practices, not only messengers.** Demonstration plots, independent assessment, and transparent performance records help farmers distinguish resilient practices from fragile ones.
- **Protect reputation.** Repeated relationships and visible feedback make misleading advice more costly.
- **Design rewards carefully.** Payments based only on successful outcomes can encourage promotion while leaving adopters exposed to losses.
- **Share downside risk.** Insurance, guarantees, technical support, or delayed payments can reduce the imbalance between the leader's reward and the follower's risk.
- **Adapt advice to local conditions.** A practice that works in a normal season may still be unsuitable where extreme production conditions are important.

## What the paper does—and does not—show

This is a theoretical paper. It does not claim that local leaders routinely mislead other farmers, and it does not estimate the size of this problem in a particular country or program.

Its contribution is to isolate a mechanism that can be tested in future field research: peer advice may fail even among otherwise similar farmers when one farmer privately knows the practice's quality, rewards depend on successful adoption, and the adopting farmer bears most of the downside risk.

The broader message is simple: **spreading sustainable technology requires more than providing information. It also requires institutions that make good advice credible.**
