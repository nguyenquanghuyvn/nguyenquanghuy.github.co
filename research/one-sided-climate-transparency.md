### Can better climate information make us less resilient?

Banks, investors, and property owners increasingly have access to detailed information about floods, storms, heat, and other physical climate risks. Better information should help people prepare. Yet identifying a risk is not the same as financing a response to it.

This paper studies a possible mismatch in climate finance. A lender may be able to measure how exposed a building or business is while remaining unable to verify how much a particular adaptation investment—such as flood protection, cooling equipment, or structural reinforcement—will reduce future losses.

I call this situation **one-sided climate transparency**: the financial system can see the danger more clearly than it can recognize the value of resilience.

### The financing problem

Suppose new information reveals that a property faces greater climate risk than previously believed. The owner now has a stronger reason to invest in protection.

At the same time, the lender expects the property, cash flow, or collateral to be worth less after a severe event. Under a conventional loan, this can reduce the amount the lender is willing to advance.

The borrower therefore wants more adaptation precisely when less financing may be available.

### Why more precise disclosure can backfire

More accurate climate disclosure creates a wider range of risk assessments. It identifies safer assets more clearly, but it also reveals assets facing especially high risk.

When borrowers can finance the adaptation they need, this information improves decisions. When financing becomes tight at the high-risk end, however, better information can place more borrowers in situations where they need more protection but can afford less of it.

In that case, a more informative disclosure system can increase expected physical losses even when everyone interprets the information correctly and lenders price risk competitively.

![Illustration of how the contract changes the value of better climate information.]({{ '/assets/images/research/one-sided-climate-transparency/value-of-disclosure.png' | relative_url }})

*Under constrained conventional lending, more informative risk assessments can raise expected losses. An integrated contract that keeps adaptation financeable restores the usual benefit of better information.*

This is not an argument against climate disclosure. The point is that **disclosure and financing design should be considered together**.

### A possible contractual response

The paper combines two tools.

**Verified adaptation** creates evidence that a resilience investment has been properly designed, installed, and maintained. This allows more of its expected benefit to support the loan.

**State-contingent repayment** reduces the amount due after a severe climate event and shifts more repayment toward the milder state, when the borrower is better able to pay.

These tools are complements. The same engineering reports, monitoring records, and agreed indicators that document adaptation can also make the repayment trigger more credible and easier to enforce. Their joint use may therefore support a viable loan even when verification alone or flexible repayment alone would not be worthwhile.

### The remaining inequality problem

A better contract does not guarantee equal access. Verification may require an up-front certification fee, and lenders may still require borrowers to contribute some of their own money.

Two otherwise identical borrowers can therefore receive different opportunities solely because one has more liquid wealth. As climate risk rises, the wealthier borrower may increase adaptation while the constrained borrower is forced to reduce it.

### What this means for policy

The model points to different policies for different constraints:

- **Shared verification standards and data** can reduce the cost of documenting resilience and make evidence reusable across lenders.
- **Targeted guarantees** can help when adaptation produces real value but too little of it can be pledged to the lender.
- **Fee support, grants, or co-financing** are needed when borrowers cannot pay certification costs or the required contribution.
- **Direct public finance** remains necessary when adaptation mainly protects public goods, communities, ecosystems, or other benefits that cannot generate private repayment capacity.

These instruments are not interchangeable. A guarantee improves the lender's position but may not give a cash-constrained borrower the money needed to enter the contract.

### What the paper does—and does not—show

This is a theoretical paper. It develops a one-period borrower–lender model and uses numerical illustrations to explain the mechanisms. No empirical data are used.

The results do not imply that all climate disclosure is harmful or that state-contingent lending is always desirable. They identify conditions under which a financial system may become better at pricing climate exposure without becoming better at financing adaptation.

### The main message

Better climate-risk information is most useful when exposed households and businesses can act on it. A system that reveals vulnerability but cannot recognize and finance credible resilience investments may become **more informed and less resilient at the same time**.

The policy challenge is therefore not disclosure alone. It is building verification and financing arrangements that allow borrowers to respond to what disclosure reveals.
