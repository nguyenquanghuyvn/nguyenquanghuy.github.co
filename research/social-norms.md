## Do other people influence what we eat?

Food choices are personal, but they are rarely made in isolation. Family members, friends, colleagues, and wider social groups help shape what people view as normal, desirable, or responsible.

This paper asks a practical question: **Are consumers more likely to choose organic food when they believe that people around them approve of it or choose it themselves?**

To answer this question, we systematically reviewed international research on social norms and organic-food choice. The evidence covers studies published between 2002 and 2025, and the reported results are converted to a common statistical scale.

## What are social norms?

Social norms can influence behavior in several ways:

- **Descriptive norms** concern what other people appear to do.
- **Injunctive norms** concern what other people approve or disapprove of.
- **Subjective norms** concern the social pressure a person feels from people who matter to them.
- **Personal moral norms** concern an individual's sense of obligation.

These ideas are related, but they are not identical. A shopper may believe that friends support organic food without seeing many of them buy it. Someone else may personally feel that buying sustainably is the right thing to do, even without direct social pressure.

## What the evidence shows

Across the studies, social norms have a **positive but moderate association** with organic-food choice. The average partial correlation is approximately **0.16**.

In plain language, people who perceive stronger social support for organic food tend to report stronger intentions to buy it. The relationship is meaningful, but it is not large enough to explain consumer behavior by itself.

The result remains similar when the analysis:

- gives each study more equal weight;
- focuses on larger or better-reported studies;
- excludes indirect and unusual estimates;
- adjusts for multiple estimates from the same study; and
- removes potentially influential observations.

## A major gap: intention is not the same as purchase

Most of the available evidence concerns **purchase intentions**, not observed behavior. Of the effect-size estimates mapped in the paper, 103 examine subjective norms in relation to purchase intention. Only a small number study actual purchases or repeated consumption.

![Most estimates study subjective norms and purchase intentions.]({{ '/assets/images/research/social-norms/evidence-concentration.png' | relative_url }})

*The research base is heavily concentrated on what consumers say they intend to do. Evidence on what they actually buy remains limited.*

This matters because consumers may express support for organic food but still buy conventional products when organic alternatives are expensive, unavailable, difficult to identify, or not trusted.

## Why social norms are not enough

Norm-based messages can make organic food appear socially accepted and morally legitimate. However, communication alone cannot remove practical barriers.

A consumer may want to buy organic food and still decide against it because of:

- higher prices;
- limited product availability;
- doubts about certification;
- uncertainty about environmental claims; or
- established shopping habits.

Social influence is therefore best understood as one part of a wider food system.

## Implications for policy and business

Campaigns that emphasize peer behavior or social approval may support sustainable consumption, but they are unlikely to work well on their own.

More effective strategies should combine social influence with:

- credible and transparent certification;
- trustworthy product information;
- reliable access in ordinary shops;
- affordable prices; and
- visible participation by relevant social groups.

For farmers and rural communities, stronger consumer demand creates opportunities only when supply chains transmit part of the value back to producers. Certification support, fair contracts, and transparent trade relationships remain essential.

## What research should do next

Future studies should measure actual purchases, repeated consumption, and changes over time—not only stated intentions. Experiments and longitudinal studies would help determine whether norm-based interventions cause lasting behavioral change.

Researchers should also distinguish more carefully between different kinds of norms and report exactly who provides the social reference: family, friends, colleagues, community members, or society more broadly.

## The main message

Social norms matter for organic-food choice, but their influence is moderate and depends on the surrounding market. People may be more open to organic food when it is socially supported, yet durable change also requires products that are credible, accessible, and affordable.
