## Why demonstration farms may give a misleading picture

Agricultural extension programs often ask a small number of farmers to test a new practice and then share the results with others. This approach makes an unfamiliar technology visible and allows farmers to learn from local experience.

But the farmers who volunteer first may be unusual. They may have better land, more money, greater confidence, stronger management skills, or a greater ability to absorb a failed trial. Their results can be measured honestly and still provide a poor guide to what other farmers should expect.

This paper asks a basic question: **Who should experiment first if the goal is to produce evidence that is useful for the wider farming population?**

## Demonstration evidence is selected twice

A demonstration program does not simply collect information. It helps determine:

1. **Who produces the evidence.** Not every invited farmer is willing or able to complete a trial.
2. **Which evidence travels.** After seeing the results, a messenger may communicate more intensively when the evidence looks favorable.

![The stages through which demonstration evidence is produced and disseminated.]({{ '/assets/images/research/who-should-experiment-first/demonstration-pathway.png' | relative_url }})

*The program first selects farmers into completed trials and then selects evidence through communication and receipt.*

This creates two separate risks. The completed demonstration farms may not represent the target population, and the results that reach the most farmers may not represent all the evidence produced.

## Why random invitations are not enough

A program may invite farmers randomly and still end up with a selected demonstration sample. The reason is voluntary completion.

Suppose a risky organic practice works especially well on certain farms. Farmers who already expect a good match may be more willing to complete the trial. Farmers with little liquidity or a high fear of losses may decline. If the program replaces each refusal until all demonstration positions are filled, it obtains the desired number of trials—but not necessarily a representative sample.

![Illustration of how voluntary completion can shift the demonstration sample.]({{ '/assets/images/research/who-should-experiment-first/selection-into-demonstration.png' | relative_url }})

*The curves are conceptual illustrations, not empirical estimates. Better compensation or verified-loss protection can move the completed sample closer to the target population, but this is not automatic.*

A fixed payment or protection against verified losses can broaden participation. Yet whether these policies improve representativeness depends on who enters at the margin. Support helps only if it brings in farmers who were previously underrepresented.

## Should the experimenter also be the messenger?

The paper compares two ways to organize a demonstration program.

In a **bundled design**, one farmer both conducts a trial and receives a reward for communicating the final evidence. This can be attractive because personal experience may command attention and reduce coordination costs.

However, the expected communication reward can also change who wants the designated experimental position. Farmers who expect favorable and easy-to-promote results may be especially eager to take it.

In a **separated design**, all experimental farmers produce evidence, while a separately appointed messenger communicates the portfolio. Communication incentives can then affect effort without directly changing which farms generate the evidence.

![Comparison of bundled and separated demonstration designs.]({{ '/assets/images/research/who-should-experiment-first/bundled-versus-separated.png' | relative_url }})

*Separation protects the evidence-producing sample from the messenger reward. Bundling may still attract more attention or cost less to organize.*

The paper does not claim that separation is always superior. Bundling may work better when farmers trust personal demonstrations much more than second-hand communication, when a separate messenger is costly, or when the program can accurately audit and correct the selection process.

## More demonstrations do not solve every problem

Repeating trials can reduce ordinary sampling uncertainty. It does not necessarily reveal how the sample was selected.

If all demonstration farms come from the same participation process, adding more observations may produce a very precise estimate for an unrepresentative group. Recruitment records and independent validation data address a different question from simple replication.

Programs should therefore record:

- every invitation and refusal;
- the characteristics of invited and participating farms;
- which contractual conditions applied to each position;
- all verified trial outcomes;
- which evidence packages were communicated;
- who received the information; and
- outcomes on farms outside the demonstration sample.

## What this means for policy

The analysis suggests several practical lessons.

**Separate experimental compensation from communication rewards.** Paying farmers to complete a difficult trial and paying someone to disseminate evidence serve different purposes.

**Protect farmers against verified trial losses.** Temporary protection can allow more financially constrained or risk-exposed farmers to participate.

**Audit recruitment, not only outcomes.** A verified result can still come from a selected sample.

**Preserve all produced evidence.** Programs should not evaluate success only through the results that were communicated most widely.

**Test portability outside the demonstration group.** Evidence is valuable only if it improves decisions for the farmers who are expected to use it.

## The main message

A good messenger is not necessarily a representative experimenter.

Demonstration programs should be designed not only to spread information, but also to produce evidence that applies beyond the farmers who are most willing, confident, or well placed to experiment first.
